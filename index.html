The Interpenetrated Histories of The French Revolution and The Marquis de Sade

The French Revolution

1789

French Revolution Begins

May 5

The Estates-General is called into session for the first time since 1614.

July 14

A mob of insurgents storms the Bastille prison, marking the start of the French Revolution.

August

L'Assemblée Nationale abolishes Feudalism and adopts The Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen

1790 June

Formal abolition of nobility and hereditary titles

1791 September

Louis XVI accepts the Constitution and is restored to power.

1792 August

Second invasion of Les Tuileries, after which the monarchy is overthrown

September

Formal abolition of the French monarchy, France declared a Republic

1793

Execution of Louis XVI

January

King Louis XVI is guillotined after being convicted of treason.

March

Revolutionary Tribunal established in France

Beginning of the Reign of Terror

September

Beginning of the Terror, a period of widespread surveillance and powers of denunciation and the execution of those suspected of treason

October

Execution of Marie Antoinette and the Girondin deputies

1794 March

Opponents of Robespierre executed

July

Arrest and execution of Maxilien Robespierre

1796 March

Marriage of Napoleon Bonaparte and Joséphine de Beauharnais

1798 September
Coup d'etat in Paris
1799 November
Bonaparte's coup d'etat removes the Directory and Council of Five Hundred and establishes a consulate with himself as First Consul.

The Life of the Marquis de Sade

1740

Donatien Alphonse François de Sade is born in Paris, France, to the Count and Countess de Sade

1763

By order of the King, Sade is imprisoned for the first time for libertinage.

1785
November

Sade completes the manuscript for "120 Days of Sodom"

Sade publishes his most famous work, "Justine, or the Misfortunes of Virtue."

1789 July

La Bastille logbook notes that Sade shouted several times from the window of his cell that the prisoners were being slaughtered and that the people should come to liberate them.

As a result of his conduct, Sade is transferred to Charenton Asylum.

1790 March

The Constituent Assembly adopts a projected decree stipulating that all prisoners detained by "lettres de cachet" will be released, thus liberating Sade.

July

Sade obtains an identity card as "an active citizen" of the Piques Section.

1791

Sade publishes the first version of "Justine".

1792

During the massacres of 10,000 prisoners, Sade is appointed the secretary of the Piques Section.

1793 November

Sade is the leader of seven other delegates who appear before the bar of the National Convention to read a petition authored by Sade.

December

Sade is imprisoned again during the Reign of Terror for a letter he had written two years earlier.

1794 October

Sade is released and authorized to live in his house on the rue Neuve-des-Mathurins, despite his former nobility.

1801

Sade is incarcerated as "administrative punishment" for having written "Justine" and Juliette".

1814

Sade dies in Charenton Asylum.
